Ring Zero · Sovereign Infrastructure · Not in primary nav — you found it

"Your community has been paying rent to landlords you've never met. Every message, every file, every relationship — sitting in infrastructure owned by someone who can change the price, the algorithm, or the terms. You already know this. That's why you're here."

Ring Zero is how a community organisation gets its own sovereign instance of Kamunity — the tools, the infrastructure, and the methodology to run it without needing us.

What you actually get

Not benefits. Not features. What exists, in plain language.

1
A forked instance that's yours

Your Kamunity. Your Supabase. Your domain. Your data. When you fork, you get the full constitutional template and the codebase. We help you configure it. After that, it runs without us. If Kamunity disappears tomorrow, your instance keeps running.

2
The constitutional floors — built in, not bolted on

Six things that can't be removed from your instance, even by you: no surveillance, data sovereignty, exit terms before entry, IP in the commons, Dunbar-limited growth, Hard-to-Love Fund allocation. These aren't policies. They're architecture. The door can't be locked because it was built without a lock.

3
Capability transfer, not dependency creation

The onboarding is designed to make you not need us. Six weeks. Constitution → Fork → Configure → Test → Launch → Independence. We're not a vendor you subscribe to. We're methodology you absorb.

4
Federation without merger

Once you're running, your instance can connect to others in the network — share signals, cross-reference resources, coordinate on shared issues — without your data leaving your control. The constitutional badge means other instances trust you. Not because we certified you. Because the architecture is transparent.

Who this is for — and who it isn't

This isn't a rejection. It's information. Knowing it's not for you yet is useful.

This is for you if —
  • Community organisations with 5–150 active members — the Dunbar range where this works
  • Orgs that hold sensitive community data and care who owns it
  • Orgs tired of paying rent to platforms that don't share their values
  • Orgs with at least one person willing to learn to maintain it — it's not hard, but someone needs to care
This is not for you if —
  • You're looking for a plug-and-play SaaS — this requires ownership, not subscription
  • You need something live in 48 hours — constitutional infrastructure takes six weeks
  • You're a large institution looking to scale extraction with better branding — the constitutional floors will reject this organically
