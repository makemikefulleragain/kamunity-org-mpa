AI Safety Checklist

5 Things to Check Before Using AI
in Your Community Organisation

AI tools can be genuinely useful for community organisations — but they come with real risks that affect your clients, your data, and your accountability. This checklist takes 2 minutes to run through before you start.

1
Data What personal data are you sharing?

Client names, health information, financial details, and case notes should never go into public AI tools (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini) without explicit consent. Check your privacy policy. When in doubt, anonymise first — replace real names with "Client A", remove postcodes, omit identifying details.

2
Accuracy Is the AI making things up?

AI tools confidently produce plausible-sounding incorrect information — wrong statistics, invented citations, outdated legislation, fabricated services. Never submit AI-generated content to funders, regulators, or media without verifying every factual claim against a primary source. Always check: who said that? Where's the original?

3
Values Does it reflect your community's values?

AI reflects the biases in its training data. For CALD communities, First Nations engagement, disability inclusion, or trauma-informed practice — AI-generated content needs careful human review. It may use inappropriate language, miss cultural context, or default to majority-culture assumptions. Your values override the AI's defaults.

4
Legal Who owns the output — and what are the terms?

AI-generated content has unclear copyright status in Australia. Before publishing, grant-reporting, or submitting AI-assisted work professionally, check: does the tool's terms of service allow commercial/NFP use? Who owns what you create? Is the output trained on copyrighted material? Read the terms — or get advice before you rely on them.

5
Governance Who's accountable when it goes wrong?

Your organisation is responsible for AI-assisted decisions — not the tool. Document when and how AI is used. Require human review for any high-stakes output: funding proposals, client assessments, public communications, financial decisions. If you can't explain how a decision was made, that's a governance gap. Have a human sign off.

🚩 Red Flags — Stop & Check
  • AI mentions a specific statistic, study, or report
  • Output will be sent to a funder, regulator, or media
  • Any real client or individual is mentioned
  • You're unsure what the tool does with your data
  • The output will affect a real person's access to services
✅ Safe Starting Points
  • Drafting internal comms (then edit heavily)
  • Brainstorming ideas — not deciding between them
  • Summarising your own documents you've already written
  • Creating templates with no personal data
  • Exploring "what questions should I be asking?"
