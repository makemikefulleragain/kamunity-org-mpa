About Kamunity

Who built this, why it exists, and what can't be changed.

The Story

I'm Mike Fuller. I've been working in and around community organisations for 30 years — quality systems in mining and manufacturing, then disability services, then community NFPs, local government, Aboriginal Community Controlled Organisations. Long enough to see the patterns.

The pattern I kept seeing: organisations doing genuinely important work, carrying genuinely hard problems, chronically under-resourced and increasingly dependent on infrastructure they didn't own or understand. Platforms that changed terms. Tools that extracted data. Tech that was built for someone else's problem and retrofitted with good intentions.

Kamunity exists because that pattern is fixable — not by selling people better software, but by helping them understand what they have, what they need, and what sovereignty actually means in practice.

The free tools came first. Kai came when the questions got too complex for a checklist. The consulting came because some problems need someone in the room. Ring Zero came because the end state isn't dependency on Kamunity either — it's genuine infrastructure sovereignty.

I built this on Whadjuk Noongar boodja. That's not decoration. The community sector in WA serves people who are disproportionately affected by colonial infrastructure. The technology we build either perpetuates that pattern or doesn't. I'm trying for doesn't.

The Six Hard Floors

These aren't policies. They're architecture. You can't remove them from this project any more than you can remove a load-bearing wall.

1. No surveillance

No tracking. No behavioural analytics. No engagement metrics on individuals. No cookies that fingerprint you. The only data collected is what's operationally necessary to provide the service you've asked for.

2. Data sovereignty

Your data belongs to you. Every tool must answer: "Can the user leave and take their data with them?" If the answer is no, we don't build it that way.

3. Exit terms before entry

You know how to leave before you start. No lock-in by design. No vendor dependency that makes leaving painful. The code is in the commons. The methodology is yours to keep.

4. IP in the commons

Everything built here is licensed CC BY-SA 4.0. You can use it, adapt it, build on it — as long as you share alike. No proprietary capture of what community organisations collectively figured out.

5. Dunbar-limited growth

This isn't built to scale to millions. It's built for the Dunbar range — communities of 5–150 where people can actually know each other. Growth that breaks that is the wrong kind of growth.

6. Hard-to-Love Fund

A portion of consulting revenue goes to maintaining services for communities that can't pay. The communities who most need sovereign infrastructure are rarely the ones with the most money. That's not a problem to solve after you're profitable — it's a floor from day one.

A bothy is a shelter in the Scottish mountains. No one owns it. No one lives there. The door is never locked. The previous person left kindling. You leave kindling for the next one.

This is the design primitive underneath everything Kamunity builds. The door is always open. Not because it's optimising for re-engagement. Because that's what doors do.
